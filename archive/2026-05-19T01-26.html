Five Cents · One Recording · One Quiet Booth

Morgan's Edison Phonograph Parlor

No. 412 Fulton Street · Brooklyn, New York
OPEN · Tuesday through Saturday · 11 a.m. — 9 p.m.
The parlor at half past seven on a Tuesday evening — the gas turned low, the booths in use, brass warm to the touch.

Welcome, friend. My name is Henry Morgan and this is my parlor. There are eight booths here — each with its own machine, its own brass horn, its own velvet stool. I will fit a cylinder to the mandrel for you and lower the reproducer myself, that's part of the price.

What you hear inside that booth is a real human being singing or speaking — a tenor from Cincinnati, a soldier from Brooklyn, a woman who has died since the wax was cut — held in beeswax and tinfoil and given back to you. I have been doing this for eleven months and I have not gotten used to it yet. I don't expect to.

Choose a cylinder. Choose a booth. Tell us, in the book by the door, what it did to you. The recordings are the same for each visitor and they are not. That is the mystery of the thing.

— H. Morgan, prop.

A Letter from Mr. Edison

Orange, New Jersey · 14 February 1885 · Tucked behind the till

My dear Mr. Morgan,

I have your figures from January and your note about the woman who wept at the Schubert. Please write me again when such a thing happens. These letters keep me awake at the right hours.

You ask whether I think the parlor business is a fashion or a foundation. I think both, and I do not think it matters. What matters is this: until last year a person who had not been born within wagon-distance of a concert hall had no realistic way of hearing trained voices in their lifetime. Your father did not hear an opera. Your grandfather did not hear a string quartet. Your customers will. For five cents. That is the whole of it.

Charge what you must to keep the doors open. Treat the cylinders gently — they are softer than you think, especially the new browns. And keep that book of yours. The reactions are data of a kind we have never had before; a man named Mr. Bell at Volta may someday wish to read it, and so will I.

Mary's lamb is well. Send my regards to the Brooklyn ear.

Yours in earnest,
Thos. A. Edison

(Try typing another word at the parlor. The diagram cabinet, maybe.)

How a Cylinder is Made

From the Edison Works, West Orange · Notes by F. Klauser, Foreman

— illustration described above; technical notes follow —

On the Wax Itself

  • Brown wax is a blend of beeswax, carnauba, ceresin and stearic acid in proportions we adjust each season for temperature.
  • It must be poured at 78 degrees Centigrade — colder and the groove shears, warmer and the cylinder shrinks unevenly as it cools on the drum.
  • A finished brown cylinder will survive about one hundred plays before the groove flattens past the point of clear reproduction. Mr. Morgan, this is why you must not replay them on request.

On the Acoustic Horn

  • The horn is not a speaker. It is a transformer of impedance — it matches the small motion of the reproducer's mica diaphragm to the great volume of the room's air.
  • A brass horn of 14 inches' flare-diameter and an exponential taper reproduces the human baritone with the least colouration. The polished interior is for cleanliness, not for sound; the brass material is for resonance.
  • A horn that rings when struck with a fingernail will also ring when a singer hits its sympathetic frequency. This is why we void warranty on dented horns.

On Care

  • Dust the mandrel between every play. A single grain of grit will plough a furrow visible to the naked eye.
  • Keep the cylinders in their cardboard sleeves and out of direct sunlight. Beeswax has a memory and the memory is bad.

— F. Klauser, 8 January 1885
