Mrs. P.'s Notes from the Sewing Table

  1. Press as you go. A flat seam is a kind seam. Set your iron warm, not blazing, and let each seam cool before the next.
  2. Walk your pattern. Lay the tissue out the night before and read it through with a cup of coffee. You will catch the dart you missed.
  3. Buy notions all at once. Twelve buttons and a card of hook-and-eye is one trip, not three.
  4. Mark every notch. Tailor's chalk, a snip in the seam allowance, or a thread tack — pick one and stick with it.
  5. Trust the grain. If the fabric pulls funny, it's the grain telling you something. Listen before you cut.

    Bulletin — From the Pattern Desk

    The Singer in the Front Parlor

    Isaac Merritt Singer set up shop in 1851 on Centre Street in New York with a patent, a borrowed forty dollars, and a temper. By the end of that decade the company was selling machines on installment — the first big consumer good a household could buy on time — and a woman on Liberty Street could have a working machine for a dollar a week.

    By 1900 there were over a million Singers in American homes. They came in oak cabinets and treadle stands and, after the war, in pale enamel with the gold scrollwork on the bed. They are still here. Half the machines in this neighborhood are Singers. The other half wish they were.

    "A pattern is a small generosity, passed hand to hand. When I sketch the cover illustration I am not thinking about Seventh Avenue or what the magazines say. I am thinking about a woman in Mansfield, Ohio, sitting down on a Tuesday evening with the pattern smoothed flat and the radio on low. If she finishes that dress and walks into church on Sunday feeling tall — that is the whole business. Every stitch she takes is her own. The machine just keeps time." — Margaret Holloway, senior designer, Butterick Pattern Service, Spring 1952
