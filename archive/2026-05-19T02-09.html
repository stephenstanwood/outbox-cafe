    ABOUT THIS FREQUENCY

    Radio Free Uprising is a five-frequency listener-supported community FM station broadcasting from a borrowed basement in this city. We rotate transmitters on a schedule we keep in our heads. We do not sell airtime. We do not run commercials. We are on the air because no one else on the band is reading the emergency room list in Spanish, calling the free clinics by name, playing Hector Lavoe at midnight, or telling the third-shift nurses that we know they are listening.

    Engineering by listener volunteers. Studio: a folding card table, two Technics turntables, a Mackie board borrowed from a church, and a transmitter built last winter from a kit and a lot of patience.

    FROM THE FOUNDER — TAPED TO THE BACK OF THE NOTICE

    To whoever is reading this —

    If you found this it means you went looking, which is more than most. I want to tell you why we keep going even though this kind of letter sits on my kitchen table about once a quarter now.

    In the fall of 1982 I started carrying a transmitter down into the basement of my cousin's auto body shop on 178th Street and broadcasting on 91.3, because nobody on commercial radio was talking to my mother. Nobody was reading the emergency room list in Spanish. Nobody was telling the women on my block where the free clinic moved to after they shut the one on Fort Washington. Nobody was playing the music anyone I love listens to. Top 40 is not for us.

    The Communications Act of 1934 says the airwaves belong to the public. We are the public. The FCC will not give a community license to a Black, Brown, queer, working-class collective in this part of this city. They will give it to a man who owns three other stations across the river. So we built our own thing. The first transmitter was confiscated in November 1983. The second one we built in six weeks with parts donated by ham operators from three boroughs. The third one is on the air right now.

    Last week a listener called the studio at 4 in the morning to say her brother is in the hospital and the only radio in his room reaches our frequency. She said: he smiled today when Tito played the Hector Lavoe. That is what we do here. That is the entire reason. We are not romantic about risk. We are not playing at rebellion. We are doing the work of community radio, which the public airwaves were supposed to be doing all along, for the people we love.

    If they take this transmitter we will build another one.

    — Marisol "Sol" Vega
    Radio Free Uprising · 178th & Audubon · spring 1985
