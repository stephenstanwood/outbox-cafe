Working Protocols of the Network

Adopted at the spring meeting, Pittsburgh, March 1908. Updated as circumstances warrant.

  1. Three readings daily — sunrise, solar noon (local apparent), and one hour after sunset — at minimum. A fourth, before retiring, is encouraged.
  2. Mercury column to be tapped lightly with the back of the fingernail before each reading. Aneroid units to be compared against a mercury reference at least monthly.
  3. Cloud forms reported by Howard's classification (cirrus, stratus, cumulus, nimbus) with modifying compound terms as needed. Hildebrandsson's 1896 atlas is the authority where Howard is ambiguous.
  4. Wind direction is the direction from which the wind blows. Sixteen-point compass acceptable; thirty-two preferred.
  5. Rain gauges to be sited at least four feet from any vertical surface and at least eight inches above ground. Snow boards level and clear of drift.
  6. Anomalous phenomena to be described in plain narrative prose before any attempt at classification. Describe first, name second.
  7. Postage on inter-station correspondence is borne by the writing party unless a reply is solicited, in which case the soliciting party encloses a stamped return.
  8. No reading is too small. No watcher is in this alone.

Bulletins of the Network of Pressure & Cloud Observers circulate quarterly from the recording station at Cresselton, N.C. Membership applications welcome; please enclose particulars of apparatus and a fortnight's specimen log.

On the Daily Reading

From the instructional plates of Cresswell & Sons, Cloud-Watch Apparatus, Hartford, Conn., 1906 edition.

"I have read the column at this same window for thirty-one years, and I will tell you what I have learned. The reading is not the weather. The reading is the sentence the weather has consented, this once, to speak. The instrument is the room in which that sentence is allowed to be heard. We are not measuring the sky. We are sitting still long enough that the sky thinks we are not in the room, and lets a true word fall.

I have been asked, often, what is the point of so many of us, so many separate columns in so many separate towns, all writing down the same numbers. The point is that the sky is bigger than any one window. To know what it is doing one must have many windows open at once. Each of you is a window. The Network is the room. We are sitting still together. That is the point.

Keep watching. The work is not small."
— C. F. Marvin, observer at Mt. Weather, in a letter to the Network, May 1908.
