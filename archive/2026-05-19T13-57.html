Voices from the moon are reaching Goldstone now at three-tenths of a watt incoming, decoded clean on S-band primary, the hands of fourteen controllers steady on the consoles.

Cross the dark immensity they travel one-point-three seconds at the speed of light, every word a small miracle of physics and patience and copper and care.

We keep careful watch on bus voltages, RCS pressures, IMU drift, suit telemetry — the work is the watching, and the watching is the love.

To the next shift of FIDOs, GUIDOs, and FLIGHTs who will sit at these consoles long after I am gone:

The thing I want you to remember is that this is happening right now. Two men, six hundred million miles around the sun from where they were born, are about to set down on another world, and the only thing connecting them to home is a thread of radio waves and the attention of sixteen people in a darkened room in Texas.

The job is not exciting in the way the public thinks. The job is checklists, voltage traces, propellant margins, and the discipline of saying GO when every number says go, and NO-GO when one number does not — even if it ruins your week, your year, your career. It is the most ordinary work in the world done at the highest possible attention.

And then sometimes, very rarely, a transmission comes down through the noise and you hear a voice from 238,000 miles away saying everything is fine, and your console lights are all green, and you remember that what we built here is a kind of love expressed as engineering.

Be steady. Listen carefully. Trust your crew. The work matters.

— Daniel Krause
Flight Director, White Team · 14 July 1969
