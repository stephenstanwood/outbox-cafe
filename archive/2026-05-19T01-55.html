Welcome to the Projection Hall

The mahogany cabinet stands open. The Argand burner is trimmed and burning bright. Select a slide from the cabinet at left and we shall thread it into the apparatus together.

This evening Mr. Penhallow has just finished mounting fourteen new chromatic slides; their pigments are dry, their condensing lenses polished. There is no greater pleasure in our age than the moment when a darkened hall draws its first collective breath at the throw of focused light upon canvas. We mean that earnestly. We mean every word of it.

    A Voyage Through the Heavens
    narrated by Miss Cordelia Vance, Senior Lecturer in Astronomy
    Twenty-two slides including the Orion Nebula, Jupiter and its four Galilean attendants, and a newly commissioned chromatic plate of the Aurora Borealis as witnessed last autumn over Mt. Washington. Coffee in the lower hall afterwards.
  • Saturday, the 19th of March · 8 o'clock · ladies free if accompanied
    Scenes from the Antipodes & Beyond
    narrated by Mr. Reuben Halsey, recently returned from Yokohama
    Mr. Halsey's own hand-painted slides of Japanese temple roofs, the Great Pyramid at evening, and the bazaars of Smyrna. Mr. Halsey carries each plate in a felt-lined cherrywood case and we believe you have not seen such color elsewhere.
  • Tuesday, the 22nd of March · 7 o'clock · members and guests
    Shakespeare in Light — A Midsummer Programme
    narrated by Mrs. Alma Thorne-Beckwith, with violin by Master Eli Halsey, age fourteen
    Twelve tableaux drawn from A Midsummer Night's Dream, painted by Miss Penelope Croft over the course of six months. Titania's bower is rendered in twenty distinct greens.
  • Friday, the 25th of March · 8 o'clock · benefit performance
    Wonders of the Microscope
    narrated by Dr. Ezekiel Brammer, Society Treasurer
    Slides prepared from genuine microscope photographs and then re-painted in hand for projection — diatom architecture, butterfly scales, the eye of the housefly. Proceeds to the new condensing lens fund.
  • Monday, the 4th of April · 7:30 · joint programme with the Salem Lyceum
    The Sacred Histories Illuminated
    narrated by the Reverend Atticus Penhallow, Society President
    Twenty-six biblical scenes, including the Conversion of Saint Paul (new this season — Miss Croft's most demanding commission to date) and Daniel in the Lions' Den. Hymns between slides.

What we are doing here

We are forty-seven men and women of Essex County who hold the firm conviction that focused light, passed through a hand-painted plate of glass and thrown upon a darkened wall, is the most important educational invention of our century. We do not say this lightly. We say it because we have watched a Sunday-school class of mill children stop breathing for forty seconds in front of a slide of the rings of Saturn. We say it because we have watched a man weep at Vesuvius. We say it because Miss Croft, who is twenty-three years old and was a milliner before she was a painter, can mix sixteen distinct shades of nebular blue from four pigments and she does this for the love of it.

The Society meets every other Wednesday in the upper room of the old Custom House on Water Street. Members pay two dollars per annum, which goes toward pigments, glass plates, replacement wicks, and the lantern itself, which is a magnificent biunial instrument from Messrs. Newton & Co. of London, donated in 1884 by Mr. Westcote himself.

If you have not yet attended a projection, we ask you with all sincerity: come. Bring a friend. Bring a child. Sit near the front. We are not putting on a show. We are showing you the universe with the lamps we have.

— the Membership Committee, March 1887

A letter, found tucked in the lantern case

Dear friends —

I write this on the eve of our fourth season. I am sixty-one years old and I have not yet grown accustomed to the moment the lamp catches and the first slide takes form on the wall. I doubt I ever shall.

Consider: a child in Newburyport, who has never travelled past Boston, may tonight stand inside the Colosseum at sunset. A widow may walk the slopes of Vesuvius and feel its heat from twenty feet of mahogany pew. A boy may see the moons of Jupiter clearly enough to count them. The cosmos has come down to us, and we have learned how to hand it gently to whomever will sit in our hall.

Painting these plates is hard, slow, exacting work. Operating the lamp requires a steadier hand than most suppose. None of us are paid. We do it because the world is full of wonders and most people will never read a book thick enough to learn them. A slide of the Orion Nebula projected ten feet high in a dark room is a kind of teaching no book has yet matched.

Keep the wicks trimmed. Keep the lenses clean. Keep the doors open.

— Thomas Augustus Westcote, founder, March 1887
